Critique


I will be critiquing " Code Meets Bio ", by my fellow peer, Archishma.

It came to be a coincidence that one of the past example website designs was also an "introduction to bioinformatics". Their website had some different focus, but it belong to the same genre. They aimed to introduce newcomers to bioinformatics. At the same time, they also aimed to showcase the past projects the author has accomplished. I will critique this former student's website, except their "critique page".

My major problem with the website is in its combination of topics, and I believe there is much room for improvement in terms of the central theme of the website. The author had noted

"This site is meant to serve as a friendly primer bioinformatics and an introduction to my personal research projects."

This combination between introduction to "bioinformatics" and "personal research project" is somewhat incoherent, especially when the website is only designed to contain 6 pages. Although the author has created very impressive content for both topics, the insufficient amount of total content presents the website as forcefully dividing itself into two different sections without much transition or connection.

Other than the lack of strong connection between the two topics, this combination also has a greater problem in terms of the audience. It is too narrow-casting to target an audience who are both "new to bioinformatics" and wish to learn about the "details of the research". Audiences who are "new to bioinformatics" cannot comprehend the language the author used to introduce their project. For example, the beginning of the "project description" already requires very specific knowledge:

"I began my project with two important pieces of background information: snoRNA coordinates, which are the locations of particular snoRNAs in the genome, and eCLIP reads, which are essentially pieces of the genome that could contain RBP binding sites."
Individuals who are "new to bioinformatics" will not be able to take advantage of the content in the later half of the website introducing the projects.

On the other hand, audiences who are interested in the research and can understand the writing about the research will not be interested in the very fundamentals of "what is bioinformatics". These introductions are too rudimentary for individuals who can grasp the project.

If the author wishes not to expand on the content of both sections, I would suggest separating the site into two different sites, each carrying out its specific focus. This will allow the respective audience to engage on their content of interest, while not being distracted by the other half of the website that are irrelevant to them.

In another approach, alternative to splitting the website, I would suggest the author focus on only one of the two topics. The writings for the other topic can act as supplementary material. For example, the author can modify their website to

  1. Focus on introducing newcomers to bioinformatics, and the research background can be used as an example of what a bioinformatician may do in a research lab. This would work well because the author, with their incredibly strong understanding in their research, can explain the goal and the procedure in a more simple manner. The resume can then be part of the "about page", increasing the ethos of the website.
    2. or
  2. Utilize the strong "personal research project" page and expand upon it. Make the emphasis of the website about the author and their projects. This would shift the "what is bioinformatics" section into the about page, used as an introduction to the motivation behind the author's project. It would then be nice to hear more about the projects themselves, as the author is definitely knowledgeable about.

I will now discuss the contents of the individual sub-pages. The "bioinformatics for beginner" page contains a very comprehensive outlook of bioinformatics. The author gave sufficient examples such that even the most beginner would be able to grasp the concept of bioinformatics:

"Maybe you already know of some key features you want to identify and study further. However, practically speaking, it's too time-intensive and too difficult to compare this sequence to other known sequences by hand. In order to solve these problems and find out more about the data you generated, you need to use bioinformatics."

A potential improvement on this page would be to include graphics whenever numbers are involved. The relative quantitative relationship is much better visualized via graphics than just plain text. For example, a line plot can be added to demonstrate the drastic reduction in cost for this part:

"the cost of sequencing an entire human genome— over 3 billion nucleotides— has dropped below $1000. This is a stark contrast to the cost of sequencing the human genome back in 2001, around the time the Human Genome Project was taking place, which was $100,000,000."

Though I have mentioned earlier that the "project" section is heavily background-dependent, the author has included some successful attempts to explain the context. It would be nice to see the similar format used on all other terminalagies.

The way I did this was by using relative entropy . Relative entropy is a broader mathematical concept, but in summary, it was a way I could normalize binding strength to see if an RBP was really binding to a snoRNA or not.

In this case, the "relative entropy", as a bioinformatics method, is hyperlinked to a credible review article, offering fundamental elaboration on the technique. This is a good approach in the sense that the hyperlink itself hides the explanatory content. For the already informed readers , their browsing is not interrupted, and for those who need this background, the explanation is readily available. This is a great example of how the medium is utilized for advantage, as a plain text would never achieve such effect. However, this is one of the few cases that a terminology is broken down on the website, and the website contains a lot more terminologies that can benefit with the similar treatment. In addition, this approach can be a good take to transform the "project" section into part of the "introduction to bioinformatics", as the hyperlinks can quickly fill in the gap in information for the newcomers.

In conclusion, I believe this website contains very in-depth information and the author is highly knowledgeable in introducing the content. The site can greatly benefit if it has a better central theme. With some edits to focus on just one of the two topics, the website can explore the one topic in more detail and orient for a better audience narrow-casting.
